The 60 / 30 / 10 read

Most teams automate the wrong things first. The useful frame is this: about sixty percent of what looks like an AI problem is traditional code and database work. About thirty percent is rule-based logic that a well-written pipeline handles. About ten percent is genuinely an AI problem.

For an immersive-content platform, the ten percent sits in places like asset generation assistance, room calibration from camera input, and content personalization. The sixty and thirty sit in the pipeline that moves a client's assets through composition, deployment, and hardware management without a founder in the middle. Eduba builds the orchestration layer there. Not the model work. The decisions about which layer each piece belongs on.

On the case side

A product company called Feeld, CTO Andrew Santus, ran a scoped sprint with Eduba covering a workshop, advisory calls, Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework. The output was an operating architecture that let the team scale product decisions without the CTO in every conversation. That is the same shape Ubicuity would use, adapted to an immersive-content pipeline with enterprise pharma and real estate buyers on the other end.

On the paper side

The Interpretable Context Methodology, submitted to ACM TiiS, argues that agent and pipeline context is best organized as a layered filesystem, which is exactly how Ubicuity's content pipeline should be structured so a Pfizer training module is reproducible, auditable, and deployable without the authoring team in the loop every time.
